Taking Care of a Greyhound 101

  • Depending on where you rescue your greyhound from, you will be told what to feed your dog. Typically, they recommend white rice and chicken. This is what we feed our dog.*
  • Put their food on a small stool.** - They are so tall, that it is hard for them to eat off the floor.
  • Walk them on leashes. Or take them off of it while you are outside in the area. Don't put them on leashes that run between two trees. They run so fast, that if they see a squirrel or small rabbit in the distance, they may start to chase it. Unfortunately, it's possible for them to run so fast that they break their own necks. Stick to walking them on leashes. Or, if you're comfortable with it and are very familiar with your dogs habits, you can let them off in a back yard to roam on their own, as they typically don't go too far.***
  • Keep their crates around with the door open. When they're scared or uncomfortable, many will voluntarily head into their crates.

*We do not. We feed her dog food. Shhhhh.

**Or, you can get to the point where you open your dishwasher and set the food on the open door. Perfect dog height!

***Proceed with caution. I am not guaranteeing your dog won't run away.

What to Expect When You're Expecting... A Greyhound

When my family adopted our first greyhound, we were told a lot about how take care of her, what to feed her and what not to do. It was all very helpful, so I've included many of these pointers; however, as with all new things, there were certiain items of note we had to learn on our own. Hopefully when you adopt your greyhoud, you can benefit from our mistakes...

  1. Females vs. Males
    2. An interesting thing about adopting a greyhound from a previous life or racing, is that the way they are treated impacts their inherant 'dog' skills as well as their behaviors. When in the XX, the female crates are stacked on top of the male crates. So, the girls have to jump in and out of their crates, but the boys just walk right in. This means that if you adopt a male, you will likely have to teach them how to jump. For example, when attempting to get her male dog into her SUV, a family friend had to lift her dogs upper legs into the car, and then pick up the hind legs and stick them into the car as well. They eventually learn how to on their own, but don't fault them when they stare at you blankly when a car door is opened.
  2. Chomping
    3. Greyhounds are very calm animals, but just like other dogs, they still get very excited before going on a walk, getting a treat etc. When they are, they might start to "chomp." (Note, this is not an official term, but it'll do.) It's when theY lift their nostrils up, so that you can see their front teeth, and they they make little chomping motions with their jaw. While it looks a tad weird, they're actualy quite happy when they do this, so don't be alarmed! Embrace the chomping.
  3. Stairs
    4. Most retired racing greyhounds have never been exposed to stairs before. So, they don't know how to go up or down them! Some websites suggest you get on all fours and show them or move their legs for them to show them. But, when we brought ours home for the first time, my family was more of the mindset of, "OH! She will be a downstairs dog! No need to worry about her 'dogging' up the upstairs." A week later, we found her running up and down the hallway, chomping, and seemingly quite pleased with herself. Basically... they can learn stairs on their own.
  4. Mini runs
    5. If you adopt a greyhound that has previously raced at a track, most likely they will sleep 90% of the day. You can't fault them for this, because, hey, they are retired and are enjoying the life of leisure they deserve. But, all of the greyhounds I've encountered still like to show off their skills every once in a while. So, surrounding areas permitting (ie. no cars, other animals, small children etc. around), you can let your greyhound off its leash and sprint circles in the back yard or run up and down the upstairs hallway. But, be careful, this may cause jealousy among the other neighborhood dogs.
  5. Speed eaters
    6. When you first adopt a greyhound, they will frequently astound you with their speed eating skills. This is because at the race tracks, the owners would feed them all together in the same area. So it was either eat. Or have your food be eaten. So, when feeding your new greyhound, do not interfere or make moves to grab the container while they are still eating until you are comfortable with them and they know you aren't trying to steal their food.
  6. They're supposed to be that skinny
    7. They're not being mistreated or starved -- that's just how they look!
